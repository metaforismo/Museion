# Claims to Evidence

## Authoring status

Early-secondary research-literacy sequence authored for Museion's deterministic
`Lesson` contract. It is a compact practice sequence, not a research-methods
credential or evidence that a learner can independently conduct research.

1. `claims-to-evidence-operationalize` — make a broad claim observable by
   defining an outcome, group, and recording rule.
2. `claims-to-evidence-observation-inference` — distinguish a recorded result
   from an explanation or an overextended conclusion.
3. `claims-to-evidence-falsifiable-comparison` — design a controlled comparison
   whose possible result could challenge a claim.

## Learner band and prerequisites

- Intended band: ages 12–16 / early secondary, with educator adaptation where
  language or accessibility needs require it.
- Prerequisites: read a short table or scenario; compare whole numbers and
  simple fractions; distinguish a question from an answer.
- Boundary: examples are low-stakes public settings. Learners are not asked to
  collect personal, health, disciplinary, financial, or identity data.

The current content contract cannot encode prerequisite edges or age gating.
These are authoring and facilitation constraints, not product-enforced claims.

## Pedagogical and safety model

Each lesson contains three guided deterministic steps and three practice steps.
Every step has a complete answer specification, a verified explanation, named
misconception triggers, and a three-level least-to-most hint ladder. The final
practice task in each lesson switches setting and asks for immediate, unassisted
near transfer. Practice remains an observation of this task only: it does not
prove durable mastery, general learning gains, independent research competence,
or far transfer.

The sequence uses deliberately modest language: observations can support a
bounded statement; they do not automatically establish causes, motives,
universal outcomes, or guarantees. The deterministic verifier is the sole
correctness authority. The browser projection omits answer specifications,
solutions, hint ladders, and misconception records.

## Misconception model

- **Opinion substituted for outcome:** measuring how attractive or convincing
  an intervention seems instead of the result in the claim.
- **Population drift:** replacing the stated group with creators, experts, or a
  convenient subgroup.
- **Observation–inference collapse:** treating a possible explanation as if it
  had been directly recorded.
- **Pilot overclaim:** turning a small, immediate comparison into a universal,
  durable, or causal conclusion.
- **Confirmation-only testing:** collecting a feature or endorsement instead of
  specifying an outcome that could challenge the claim.
- **Unequal conditions:** changing task difficulty, assistance, or measurement
  between comparison groups.

## Evidence limits

- A correct answer is evidence only about the supplied finite scenario.
- Immediate near-transfer tasks are not delayed retention tests.
- Nothing in this sequence measures real-world decision quality, research
  ethics competence, durable mastery, or general learning gains.
- The course intentionally avoids presenting simplified classroom comparisons
  as a substitute for formal study design, consent, review, or expert advice.
